Post Pandemic Transportation

Public Transportation in New York City After COVID-19

Before the pandemic, New York City's public transportation system carried nearly 8 million riders on an average weekday, making it one of the busiest transit networks in the world. Then in March of 2020, that number began to plummet due to the beginning of the Covid-19 Pandemic. This project uses MTA daily ridership data from March 2020 through January 2025 to examine how New York City's public transit system has recovered, how subway and bus ridership diverged in their recoveries, and what the numbers reveal about how the city itself has changed.

The Collapse (2020)

When New York State issued its stay-at-home order on March 22, 2020, subway ridership fell to levels not seen since the system opened in 1904. By April 12, the lowest recorded day in the dataset, only 198,399 people rode the subway, compared to a pre-pandemic daily average of roughly 4.9 million. Nurses, grocery clerks, and sanitation workers became the MTA's only remaining passengers almost overnight. Buses told an even starker story. By May 2020, daily bus ridership had collapsed to just over 6,000 riders, a system that once moved hundreds of thousands of New Yorkers reduced to something closer to a quiet neighborhood shuttle. Across all of 2020, the subway averaged just 26% of its pre-pandemic ridership, and buses fared little better.

"It (the subway) felt like a ghost town compared to what I've been used to my entire life leading up to it (the pandemic)." - Matthew O'Hagan, New York Resident

An Uneven Recovery (2021–2023)

Recovery was slow and uneven. The subway climbed from 26% of pre-pandemic ridership in 2020 to 46% in 2021, 61% in 2022, and 70% by 2023 — but never fully returned to its former baseline. Buses recovered faster than the subway in 2021, reaching 57% while the subway sat at 46%, but then stalled. By 2023, buses plateaued around 63–64% and actually declined slightly to 61% by 2024, while the subway continued its slow climb. The gap between them widened over time.

Subway Bus

By the end of 2023, a clear divergence had emerged between the two modes. The subway continued its gradual climb, averaging 70% of pre-pandemic ridership across the year and showing no signs of plateauing. Buses, however, told a different story. After peaking at roughly 63% recovery in 2022, bus ridership began to slip. By 2024, buses had fallen back to just 61% of their pre-pandemic baseline, nearly two percentage points below their peak. While the subway added roughly 380,000 daily riders between 2022 and 2024, buses actually lost ground, dropping by about 44,000 daily riders over the same period. The two systems, once tracking closely together, were now moving in opposite directions.

The Big Picture — Where Things Stand Now

Taken together, the subway and bus data paint a striking picture of how much ground NYC transit has yet to recover. Before the pandemic, the two systems combined for roughly 7.26 million daily riders. By 2024, that combined figure had only reached 4.38 million — just 60% of the 2019 baseline. That means nearly 2.88 million daily riders have not returned. The subway's continued climb to 72% contrasts sharply with buses, which have dipped back below their 2022 recovery peak, suggesting the two systems are on very different trajectories.

Conclusion

Five years after the pandemic brought New York City's transit system to a near-standstill, the MTA's own data tells a story that goes beyond a simple recovery. The subway has climbed back to 75% of pre-pandemic ridership, but that remaining 25% may never return. The pandemic didn't just keep people home temporarily. It permanently rewired how millions of New Yorkers think about getting around. Remote and hybrid work eliminated the daily commute for a generation of office workers, and the rise of app-based rides, rentable e-bikes, and electric scooters gave city residents more options than ever before. Buses have felt this shift most acutely, stalling at just 59% of pre-pandemic ridership. For many riders, the convenience of a Citi Bike or a quick Uber simply replaced the crosstown bus. Combined, the subway and bus systems still carry 2.88 million fewer daily riders than they did in 2019.

"Obviously things are so much different now with how many people work from home and new modes of transportation gaining popularity due to the pandemic, like electric bikes and the scooters, but the need for public transport seems a little different now." - Matthew O'Hagan, New York Resident
